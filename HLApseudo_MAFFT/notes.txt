3'UTR uniqueness — observations from hla_shared_fraction figure
================================================================

All three 3'UTRs are at zero sharing across their entire length, for all window sizes,
with any pseudogene in the group. The drop is hard and immediate at the CDS boundary.

What this means in practice:

10x Genomics 3' scRNA-seq has a structural advantage for HLA that bulk RNA-seq does not.
Reads are anchored at the poly-A tail, so they land in precisely the region that is fully
unique. A 100 bp 3' read from HLA-A will fall somewhere in positions 1117–1435 in the
figure — zero pseudogene sharing, unambiguous mapping regardless of aligner.

But this requires a UTR-inclusive reference. If the STAR index was built from CDS-only
sequences (RefSeq NM_ transcript FASTA, or Ensembl/GENCODE filtered to protein-coding
CDS), those 3'UTR k-mers are absent. Reads landing there become unmapped, not
multimapped — silent loss with no warning from the aligner. This is the most common
failure mode: tools like Cell Ranger use a pre-built genome reference (genome + GTF),
so the 3'UTR is included, but pipelines that build a transcript index from coding
sequences only will miss it entirely.

For DRB1 the margin is thinner. The 3'UTR is only 319 bp (positions 904–1222). A 100 bp
read placed anywhere in the last 100 bp of the 3'UTR is fine, but the window is narrow —
approximately 220 positions of safe territory before the CDS begins to contribute
ambiguous signal. For very short-read chemistries or trimmed reads this could become
limiting.

The sc3UTR analysis in the mappability project (compute_3prime_uf.py) is measuring
exactly this. The UF for the last 250/400/500/600 bp of HLA transcripts should be near
1.0 if the reads fall in 3'UTR, and will drop sharply when the window extends back into
the CDS. The alignment figure provides the alignment-space explanation for why.
